# Trace Relay Creative Brief

## Status and recommendation

- Creative gate status: **selected treatment, pending Islamic Content Review**.
- Game: **Trace Relay**.
- First-slice dimension: **2D**.
- Selected creative treatment: **Ribbon Relay**.
- Next required gate: **Islamic Content Review**.
- This brief defines creative intent and product behavior only. It does not select DOM, SVG, canvas, a framework, permanent architecture, score trust model, or production assets.

## Product promise

A player watches a short route travel through an irregular field of distinctive anchors, reproduces that route across three compact rounds, receives a clear score, and hands the identical hidden challenge to a friend through one language-independent link. Both users can compare fairly, rematch, or share again without registration, public ranking, humiliation, or route spoilers.

The experience should be understood in seconds, remain satisfying in silence, fit a short social-media session, work from 320px upward, and preserve equivalent decisions for touch, pointer, sequential tap, and keyboard users.

## Creative treatment comparison

The selected product opportunity and 2D decision are fixed. The following treatments explore materially different identities for the same Trace Relay mechanic.

| Treatment | Core fantasy | Field and route identity | Strengths | Main risks | Decision |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ribbon Relay** | Receive a travelling route, remember its handoff, and pass it onward | An asymmetrical field of large route markers; a broad folded ribbon visits them, then retracts before the player responds | Immediate route readability, strong relay metaphor, spoiler-safe result transformation, language-light, silent-safe, low asset cost, clear reduced-motion equivalent, unlike a regular unlock grid | Ribbon and anchor shapes require careful originality and symbol review | **Selected** |
| **Harbor Handoff** | Guide a signal between abstract floating stations | Flat island-like plates separated by negative space; a wake-like trail moves station to station | Friendly atmosphere, strong spatial memory, appealing depth-like layering while remaining 2D | Water/wake styling may add decorative complexity, reduce high-contrast clarity, and drift toward a themed world before the core loop is proven | Rejected for first slice |
| **Workshop Sequence** | Rebuild a route through a clean abstract assembly table | Interlocking tabs, sliders, and mechanical traces reveal an ordered path | Tactile identity, clear cause and effect, good input feedback opportunities | Risks resembling circuit puzzles or branded electronic memory products; more visual noise and cultural specificity | Rejected for first slice |

### Why Ribbon Relay wins

Ribbon Relay best balances clarity, fun, social meaning, accessibility, performance, Islamic-content reviewability, and originality. The route is a visible handoff rather than a security pattern or a color-and-sound sequence. Its broad line can remain readable on small screens, its anchors can communicate state through silhouette and outline rather than color alone, and its result can transform into a compact three-band relay mark without exposing the route. The treatment requires no character, clothing, realistic environment, musical dependency, or complex asset loading.

## Selected treatment: Ribbon Relay

### Core fantasy

The player is completing a digital relay. A route arrives as a broad ribbon that briefly travels through a set of abstract route markers. The player remembers the handoff and sends the same route onward by selecting the markers in order. After three rounds, the route folds into a spoiler-safe relay mark that represents the result and can be passed to a friend.

This is not a literal running relay and does not ask users to imitate a physical challenge. It is an abstract digital handoff.

### Creative vocabulary

- **Anchor field:** an irregular, non-grid arrangement of five to nine large markers with strong spacing and no phone-like 3×3 layout.
- **Anchors:** visually distinct non-devotional geometric markers with unique silhouette or internal notch patterns. Final symbols require Visual Direction and Islamic Content Review.
- **Route:** one broad ribbon that visits anchors in sequence. It may cross itself and, in round three, may revisit one earlier anchor non-consecutively.
- **Hidden state:** the ribbon fully retracts; anchors retain neutral identity but no route history.
- **Player trace:** selections create short confirmed ribbon segments only after valid input. Incorrect input does not draw a misleading route.
- **Relay mark:** three compact abstract bands representing the three rounds, used on the result and share presentation without reproducing field geometry.

The field must not use evenly spaced dots, a security-screen frame, four colored quadrants, a circular sound pad, numbered keypad styling, or a recognizable toy layout.

## Complete game loop

### Session target

- Typical first session: **30–45 seconds** including the optional visual demonstration.
- No mandatory registration.
- No hard fail that ends the whole session after one mistake.
- No countdown pressure during route observation.
- Active reproduction time may influence a small score component, but accuracy and progress remain primary.

### Round structure

| Round | Field size | Route length | Creative purpose |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| 1 | 5 anchors | 4 selections | Establish observe → hide → reproduce with generous spacing |
| 2 | 7 anchors | 5 selections | Increase visual choice while preserving a simple route |
| 3 | 9 anchors | 6 selections | Complete the relay; one non-consecutive anchor revisit may appear |

The deterministic challenge seed defines all field layouts and routes. The same seed produces the same decisions for every locale and supported input mode. The board geometry never mirrors in RTL.

### Observe phase

1. The round label and a language-light observe cue appear outside the board.
2. The route ribbon visits each anchor in sequence.
3. Every visited anchor changes in at least two ways, such as outline plus internal notch or silhouette state; color and motion are never the only signals.
4. The complete route remains visible briefly, then retracts fully.
5. Player input stays disabled until concealment is complete.

Reduced-motion presentation uses discrete state changes and short fades with the same total observation opportunity. No player receives a shorter route-viewing window because of motion preference.

### Reproduction phase

The player may use any supported interaction without entering a separate mode:

- sequential taps on touch;
- press-and-drag with forgiving anchor snapping;
- sequential pointer clicks;
- keyboard spatial navigation or deterministic focus traversal plus confirmation.

The route is judged by selected anchor order, not by drawing precision between anchors. Drag distance, pointer path smoothness, swipe velocity, and handwriting-like accuracy do not affect score.

### Correct input

- The selected anchor receives immediate non-color-only confirmation.
- A short ribbon segment locks from the previous correct anchor.
- Progress advances by one route position.
- Silent play remains complete; any future audio is optional and separately reviewed.

### Incorrect input and assistance

- A wrong selection does not erase completed progress and does not restart the round.
- The incorrect anchor receives a brief restrained error state; no full-screen shake, flashing, alarm, humiliation, or punitive wording.
- The player remains on the same expected route position and can correct immediately.
- After two wrong attempts on the same position or a meaningful idle interval, an optional **show next** assistance action becomes available.
- Using assistance reveals only the next required anchor, marks that position as assisted, and removes first-try mastery credit for that position while preserving progress.
- The player can choose **finish now** at any time and receive a partial result rather than being trapped.

### Round completion

- The completed player trace settles into one compact band.
- The band joins the round indicator.
- A short transition prepares the next field without retaining previous anchors or input state.
- In reduced motion, the completed band appears through an instantaneous or minimal-fade state change.

### Session completion

After round three, the three round bands align into the relay mark. The score and three plain-language dimensions appear:

- mastery;
- progress;
- flow.

The route, anchor positions, field layout, and seed solution are not displayed on the result or share surface.

## Score composition and tie behavior

The creative score is a maximum of **1000 points**:

- **Mastery — 700 points:** proportional to route positions selected correctly on the first attempt without assistance.
- **Progress — 200 points:** proportional to route positions ultimately completed, including corrected or assisted positions.
- **Flow — 100 points:** a capped bonus based on active reproduction time after the route is hidden. Thresholds must be generous enough that every supported input method can reasonably earn the full bonus.

Speed can never compensate for substantially lower mastery. Observation time, pause time caused by the page being hidden, reduced-motion preference, localization direction, and optional-instruction viewing do not reduce score.

Tie order:

1. higher total score;
2. higher mastery;
3. more completed route positions;
4. lower active reproduction time;
5. explicit shared tie when all compared values match.

The architecture gate must validate the exact formula, time measurement, pause rules, input-mode fairness, and score-claim trust model without changing this priority order.

## State-by-state user journey

### 1. Discovery entry

The user sees:

- a compact Trace Relay card;
- a static or restrained micro-demonstration of a ribbon visiting several irregular anchors;
- one primary start action;
- a short duration signal such as “about 40 seconds,” localized later.

The card contains no long explanation, route spoiler, public leaderboard, character, or culture-specific idiom.

The user understands: watch the route, repeat it, pass the same challenge to a friend.

### 2. First-time demonstration

A short skippable demonstration shows:

1. three anchors;
2. a two-step route reveal;
3. the ribbon hiding;
4. two example selections;
5. a compact result cue.

The demonstration uses icons and minimal localized verbs. It can be replayed from optional help. Returning players proceed directly to the first round unless they request it.

### 3. Direct shared-link entry

The recipient sees:

- a friendly score to beat, without a sender name requirement;
- a generic relay mark, never the route;
- confirmation that both players receive the same challenge;
- one primary play action;
- safe handling when the link is invalid, expired by future policy, or unsupported.

Invalid or unavailable links fall back to a normal new Trace Relay session with a clear localized explanation. The product never exposes raw seed data in user-facing copy.

### 4. Active play

For each round the user:

- observes;
- waits for the route to hide;
- reproduces by the available input method;
- corrects mistakes without a full reset;
- may request one-step assistance;
- completes or finishes early.

The HUD remains minimal: round, route-position progress, optional help, and finish control. Score does not continuously animate during observation because that distracts from memory.

### 5. Partial success

A user who finishes early, leaves a route incomplete, or uses assistance still receives:

- completed positions;
- mastery achieved;
- a friendly improvement cue;
- replay and share options only when the resulting challenge state is valid.

Copy must describe the result without failure labels such as “loser,” “weak,” “bad memory,” or “embarrassing.”

### 6. Result

The result presents:

- total score out of 1000;
- mastery, progress, and flow;
- completed rounds or positions;
- replay;
- challenge a friend;
- optional concise explanation of how the score was formed.

The relay mark becomes the share-worthy moment. It communicates completion and score tier through band count, outline, and shape treatment—not color alone.

### 7. Replay

Replay creates a clean new run from the real product state:

- no retained ribbon segments;
- no duplicate input handlers;
- no old timers or effects;
- no hidden score carryover;
- the same challenge may be replayed for personal improvement when product rules allow;
- a new challenge uses a new deterministic seed.

### 8. Friend comparison

After the friend finishes, the comparison shows both results with equal visual weight:

- total score;
- mastery;
- progress;
- flow;
- tie or lead statement;
- rematch;
- share again.

Friendly comparative language is required. Examples of tone intent, not production translations:

- “You led on mastery.”
- “Your scores matched.”
- “A new relay is ready.”

Avoid “destroyed,” “crushed,” “humiliated,” “revenge,” public ranking, streak pressure, countdown threats, or repeated notification pressure.

### 9. Rematch and share-again

- **Rematch:** create a fresh deterministic route and a new language-independent challenge link.
- **Share again:** share the current result and challenge entry without revealing the route.
- The product must not auto-post, access contacts without explicit action, or imply that sharing is required to continue.

## Spoiler-safe share presentation

### Share-worthy result moment

At completion, the active route retracts and folds into three stacked, non-directional bands. The bands settle around or beside the score to form the relay mark. This transformation is the main share-worthy moment because it feels earned but contains no route information.

### Share card content

Allowed:

- Trace Relay identity;
- total score;
- mastery/progress/flow summary;
- completion state;
- generic relay mark;
- friendly challenge invitation;
- language-independent link.

Forbidden:

- anchor field layout;
- route segments;
- visited-anchor order;
- seed value;
- solution preview;
- screenshot of active play;
- sender personal data by default;
- humiliating comparison copy;
- fake urgency.

Link-preview imagery must remain generic even when the URL identifies a specific challenge.

## Input and accessibility contract

### Equivalent decisions

All supported users must make the same ordered-anchor decisions. No input method receives unique hints, shorter routes, different geometry, or a higher score ceiling.

- Touch sequential tap is first-class.
- Touch drag is optional and forgiving, not precision-scored.
- Pointer sequential click is first-class.
- Keyboard navigation and confirmation are first-class.
- Visible focus is always required.
- Anchor hit areas remain large and separated at 320px.

### Non-color, non-sound, and non-motion communication

Each gameplay state must have at least two visual or semantic channels:

- silhouette or outline;
- internal notch or pattern;
- line state;
- textual or accessible announcement;
- optional color;
- optional reviewed sound.

The game cannot depend on hue recognition, audio recognition, rapid animation, or drag dexterity.

### Accessible naming

Each anchor requires a stable, deterministic accessible name based on its marker identity and field position. Final naming must be concise in Arabic, English, and Turkish and must not rely on left/right wording that becomes ambiguous under RTL. The board itself remains geometrically unchanged across locales.

### Interruption and recovery

- Page hiding or interruption pauses active reproduction timing.
- On return, the user receives a clear continue or restart-current-round choice.
- Restarting the current round must not silently preserve partial trace state.
- Reduced motion preserves route order, dwell opportunity, decisions, assistance, scoring ceiling, and comparison meaning.

## Multilingual and RTL constraints

Arabic RTL, English, and Turkish are mandatory from the first implementation architecture.

- Gameplay geometry, anchor IDs, route order, deterministic seed, and scoring rules are language-independent.
- The board does not mirror in RTL.
- Shell hierarchy, text alignment, action order, result layout, comparison layout, and accessible announcements follow locale direction.
- The challenge URL contains no locale and resolves using a safe local preference or product fallback.
- No text is embedded in artwork.
- Avoid wordplay, reading-speed mechanics, culture-specific metaphors, and long mandatory instructions.
- Mixed-direction links and identifiers require directional isolation.
- Score, time, decimal separators, digit shaping, punctuation, and round notation require locale-specific validation.
- Long labels must wrap outside the active anchor field and never cover anchors.

Required copy intents, not final translations:

- Start;
- Watch;
- Your turn;
- Round 1 of 3;
- Show next;
- Finish now;
- Score;
- Mastery;
- Progress;
- Flow;
- Replay;
- Challenge a friend;
- Compare;
- Rematch;
- Share again;
- Invalid challenge fallback;
- Paused session recovery.

## Islamic-content review packet

### Proposed content

- Theme: abstract digital handoff using geometry and ribbon-like route graphics.
- Characters and clothing: none.
- Environment: neutral abstract field; no sacred, ritual, occult, devotional, or realistic hazardous setting.
- Symbols: non-devotional geometric anchors only; final glyph inventory requires review to avoid religious or occult resemblance.
- Audio: not required. The experience must be complete in silence.
- Rewards: score, progress, replay, and friendly comparison only.
- Monetization: none in this slice.
- Chance mechanics: deterministic challenge generation; no wager, betting, loot box, monetary chance reward, or purchasable advantage.
- Social pressure: private asynchronous invitation, no public leaderboard, public feed, humiliation, contact harvesting, or forced sharing.
- Physical safety: no real-world imitation or physical challenge.

### Questions for Islamic Content Review

1. Confirm whether the abstract ribbon and proposed geometric marker family are acceptable once Visual Direction provides the exact glyph sheet.
2. Confirm that the three-band completion transformation carries no problematic symbolic resemblance.
3. Decide whether any non-musical interface tones may be used; implementation must remain silent by default unless the approved policy permits a defined approach.
4. Review final celebration particles or fragments, if any, for symbolic and excess concerns.
5. Confirm the friendly challenge and comparison framing does not introduce prohibited humiliation, manipulation, or harmful pressure.

No religious PASS is claimed by this brief.

## Originality teardown

### Distinction from phone unlock patterns

Trace Relay must differ structurally and visually:

- irregular field, never a standard 3×3 grid;
- large varied markers, never identical dots;
- observe-and-reproduce game context, never security or unlocking language;
- sequential tap and keyboard are first-class, not continuous drag only;
- route may cross itself and may revisit an anchor in round three;
- three-round mastery/progress/flow result rather than binary unlock success;
- private asynchronous same-seed comparison and rematch;
- relay mark share result that hides the route.

### Distinction from Simon-style products

- no four-color quadrant layout;
- no cumulative one-more-step loop;
- no sound-dependent sequence;
- no branded color order or circular pad;
- no single-error elimination;
- spatial field changes across rounds;
- identical seeded friend challenge and structured comparison.

### Distinction from existing memory toys and route games

- the identity is a route handoff through irregular generated markers, not imitation of a known board, toy, character, branded palette, sound, level, or trade dress;
- partial progress and assistance are preserved rather than abrupt elimination;
- share output intentionally conceals the solution;
- multilingual and accessible input equivalence are part of the concept, not later overlays.

### Rejection list

Do not use:

- 3×3 dot grids;
- lock, password, unlock, security, or authentication metaphors;
- four large colored pads;
- repeating musical note sequences;
- copied toy sounds;
- circular console layouts;
- branded neon palettes or familiar logos;
- hearts, lives, fail buzzers, skulls, flames, crowns, or humiliating rank labels;
- known characters, mascots, levels, screenshots, or copied wording;
- public leaderboards, streak threats, revenge language, or forced invitations.

Before implementation, Motion Design must resolve:

1. route reveal cadence and total observation window;
2. concealment timing and input-enable boundary;
3. immediate input confirmation;
4. correct-segment lock behavior;
5. restrained wrong-input and near-miss feedback;
6. assistance reveal;
7. round completion and next-round transition;
8. final relay-mark transformation;
9. comparison and rematch transition;
10. reduced-motion equivalents with equal information and scoring opportunity;
11. bounded particle/effect budgets;
12. replay reset, navigation teardown, pause, and resume behavior.

No flashing, full-screen shake, motion-only instruction, punitive failure animation, distracting idle loop, or unbounded particles.

## Feasibility and performance constraints

- Preserve 2D for the first slice; depth-like layering may be visual only.
- Use at most nine active anchors and one active route presentation per round.
- Prefer repository-owned procedural or lightweight vector-style assets; final rendering choice belongs to architecture review.
- First meaningful interaction target: within 2.5 seconds on the representative budget Android profile over ordinary 4G.
- Active-play target: stable 30 fps minimum and 60 fps on capable devices.
- Keep effects bounded, removable, and silent-safe.
- Provide a reduced-detail fallback without changing geometry, decisions, route visibility, state clarity, or score ceiling.
- The implementation must support clean pause, replay, navigation teardown, and repeated sessions without duplicate handlers, timers, effects, or retained board state.

## Creative risks and mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation required before implementation |
|---|---|
| Resembles an unlock pattern | Enforce irregular non-grid field, varied marker silhouettes, relay vocabulary, sequential-tap/keyboard emphasis, route revisit, and forbidden-reference review |
| Route is hard to perceive on a small screen | Use broad route, large anchors, limited field size, strong spacing, complete-route hold, and high-contrast state pairs |
| Speed scoring disadvantages keyboard or motor-impaired users | Keep flow at 10%, use generous capped thresholds, pause correctly, and validate full score access across input modes |
| Assistance feels punitive | Preserve progress, remove only first-try mastery for the assisted step, and use neutral wording |
| Shared result spoils route | Never include field, route, anchor order, seed, active-play screenshot, or specific link-preview solution |
| Arabic RTL changes challenge fairness | Never mirror board geometry or route order; adapt only shell and text direction |
| Celebration introduces uncertain symbols or audio | Route exact visuals, particles, glyphs, and all audio decisions through Islamic Content Review |
| Scope expands into a generic engine | Implement only the product contracts required by this complete vertical slice |
